04 How It Works

Built on data.
Honest about its limits.

Understanding what Beba Salama does — and what it cannot do — is as important as the findings. Transparency is not optional when the subject is human safety.

📡
Two Data Sources, Combined

Layer 1 — World Bank/Ma3Route: 31,064 geolocated crash reports from the Ma3Route crowdsourced platform, August 2012 to July 2023. Every crash carries a GPS coordinate, timestamp, and contextual flags for fatalities, matatus, boda bodas, and pedestrians derived from tweet text.

Layer 2 — Kenya Police/HDX: 1,118 ground-level police incident records from 2016–2017 with road names, cause codes, victim types, and county breakdowns — used for route-level enrichment and validation of Layer 1 findings.

⚖️
The Severity Score

No single column captures crash severity. We engineer a composite score from available signals — weighted to amplify the most serious incidents.

Severity Score =
+5 × fatality flag    (dominant weight)
+3 × pedestrian involved
+2 × matatu involved
+2 × motorcycle involved
+2 × independent report count
Score ≥ 5 → classified HIGH RISK · 16.8% of all crashes
🗺️
The Risk Checker Model

The route risk score combines four factors — calibrated against eleven years of crash patterns.

Route danger index (35%) — crash density per corridor
Time of day risk (30%) — hourly severity profile
Vehicle vulnerability (25%) — relative injury exposure by mode
Day type factor (10%) — weekly and holiday patterns

This is a calibrated heuristic model, not yet the trained LightGBM classifier in active development. Scores are data-grounded and directionally accurate — treat them as risk signals, not precise predictions.

🎯
Who Pays for This

Passengers — free web tool. Check route risk before boarding with time-of-day awareness most Kenyan commuters have never had access to.

PSV Insurers — B2B SaaS. Route-level risk scoring as an underwriting input for pricing public transport vehicle coverage more precisely than vehicle age alone. This is the revenue model.

NTSA and Government — policy tool. Evidence-based corridor identification for enforcement deployment and infrastructure investment prioritisation. Long-term partnership play.

⚠ What this platform does not know
  • Crowdsourced bias: Layer 1 depends on who tweets. Urban, English-literate, smartphone-connected reporters are over-represented. Rural and lower-income routes are likely under-counted — not lower-risk.
  • Partial calendar coverage: Layer 2 covers 3 months of 2016 and 10 months of 2017. Seasonal claims from that layer are directional, not definitive.
  • Engineered severity, not ground truth: "High risk" is a composite model signal, not an official incident classification. It should be read as an indicator, not a fact.
  • Consequences of being wrong: A route flagged high risk affects real operators' livelihoods and real passengers' decisions. Any production deployment needs confidence intervals, a feedback mechanism, and clear accountability when predictions fail.
  • Accessibility gap: A risk score is only useful to someone with smartphone access and data. The platform must be judged on whether it reaches the pedestrians and boda riders who carry the highest risk — not just those who already own smartphones.
05 Key Findings

Six things the data reveals
about Kenya's roads.

Not assumptions. Each finding is derived directly from 31,064 crash records spanning eleven years of Kenyan roads.

01
Peak severity: 05:00Counter-intuitive
5am is the deadliest hour — not midnight

Conventional wisdom assumes late night is most dangerous. The data disagrees. 5am registers the highest average crash severity of any hour — when long-distance trucks completing overnight runs, early matatus, and boda bodas converge on empty roads with maximally fatigued drivers and near-zero emergency response capacity.

02
Night: 11.0% fatalDay: 6.9% fatal
A night crash is 60% more likely to kill you

The gap is structural, not marginal. Lower visibility, higher speeds on quieter roads, delayed emergency response, and driver fatigue compound into a 60% elevation in mortality probability. Every journey between 9pm and 4am carries this invisible premium that most Kenyan commuters are never told about.

03
Sunday: highest fatality rateFriday: highest volume
Sunday kills more. Friday crashes more. Two different problems.

Friday carries the highest raw crash volume — end-of-week travel, social commuting, school runs. Sunday's lower volume conceals a dramatically higher fatality rate, driven by long-distance inter-county journeys, alcohol involvement, and reduced medical access in rural areas. High volume and high lethality require different interventions.

04
2,541 crashes8.2% of all incidents
Matatus carry a disproportionate share of Kenya's road death

Public service vehicles appear in 8.2% of all recorded crash events. Given 14 seats per matatu, each incident carries a fundamentally different human cost than a private vehicle crash. The dataset cannot fully capture this multiplier effect — the true human cost is higher than the incident count implies.

05
Peak: 6,101 in 2015Recent: 1,805 in 2022
Crash volume has fallen since 2015 — but the work is far from done

A meaningful downward trend from the 2015 peak reflects road safety interventions, infrastructure improvements, and enforcement campaigns. However, 1,805 recorded incidents in 2022 — in a dataset that captures only a fraction of total crashes — means progress and crisis coexist.

06
5,206 high-risk events16.8% of crashes
One in six crashes is predictable before it happens

Using a composite severity model — weighting fatality indicators, pedestrian involvement, vehicle type, and corroborating report volume — 16.8% of all crashes carry a high-severity classification. Critically, all inputs (time, location, vehicle type, day) are known before the journey begins. Risk is not random. It is predictable — and therefore preventable. This is the core insight Beba Salama is built on.
