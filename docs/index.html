04 Data Findings

Six things the data reveals
about Kenya's roads.

Not assumptions. Each finding is derived directly from 31,064 crash records across eleven years.

01
Peak severity: 05:00Counter-intuitive
5am is the deadliest hour — not midnight

Conventional wisdom assumes late night carries the most danger. The data disagrees. 5am registers the highest average crash severity of any hour — when long-distance trucks completing overnight runs, early matatus, and boda bodas converge on empty roads with maximally fatigued drivers and near-zero emergency response capacity.

02
Night: 11.0% fatalDay: 6.9% fatal
A night crash is 60% more likely to kill you

The gap between night and day fatality rates is structural, not marginal. Lower visibility, higher speeds on quieter roads, delayed emergency response, and driver fatigue compound into a 60% elevation in mortality probability. Every journey between 9pm and 4am carries this invisible premium.

03
Sunday: highest fatality rateFriday: highest volume
Sunday kills more. Friday crashes more. Two different problems.

Friday carries the highest raw crash volume — end-of-week travel, social commuting, school runs. But Sunday's lower volume conceals a dramatically higher fatality rate, driven by long-distance inter-county journeys, alcohol involvement, and reduced medical facility access in rural areas. High volume and high lethality require different interventions.

04
2,541 crashes8.2% of all incidents
Matatus carry a disproportionate share of Kenya's road death

Public service vehicles appear in 8.2% of all recorded crash events. Given their passenger capacity — 14 seats per matatu — each incident carries a fundamentally different human cost than a private vehicle crash. The dataset cannot fully capture this multiplier effect; the true human cost is higher than the incident count implies.

05
Peak: 6,101 in 2015Recent: 1,805 in 2022
Crash volume has fallen since 2015 — but the problem remains acute

The data shows a pronounced peak in 2015 followed by a meaningful downward trend reflecting road safety interventions, infrastructure improvements, and enforcement campaigns. However, 1,805 recorded incidents in 2022 in a dataset that captures only a fraction of total crashes means progress and crisis coexist.

06
5,206 high-risk events16.8% of crashes
One in six crashes is predictable before it happens

Using a composite severity model — weighting fatality indicators, pedestrian involvement, vehicle type, and corroborating report volume — 16.8% of all crashes carry a high-severity classification. Critically, all inputs to this classification (time, location, vehicle type, day) are known before the journey begins. Risk is not random. It is predictable — and therefore preventable. This is the core insight Beba Salama is built on.
